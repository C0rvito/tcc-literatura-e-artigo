Abstract

36

Mutations in the gene glucosidase, beta acid 1 (GBA1) are the strongest genetic risk

37

factor for Parkinson’s disease (PD) and are associated with faster disease progression.

38

GBA1 is expressed in all cell types of the central nervous system, with some evidence

39

supporting higher expression in glial cells than neurons. To elucidate possible specific

40

functions in neurons versus glia, we differentiated human induced pluripotent stem cells

41

(iPSCs) generated from an individual with PD heterozygous for the GBA1 pathogenic

42

null variant IVS2+1 (GBA1IVS/+), homozygous GBA1 IVS2+1 isogenic to GBA1IVS/+

43

(GBA1IVS/IVS) and a healthy unaffected age- and sex-matched individual (GBA1+/+).

44

GBA1 expression was reduced in GBA1IVS/+ and GBA1IVS/IVS neurons and astrocytes.

45

Endolysosomal trafficking was significantly altered in GBA1-deficient neurons with

46

enlarged early and recycling endosome and lysosome compartments in neurons but not

47

in astrocytes. High molecular weight oligomerization of -synuclein and phosphorylated

48

Ser129 -synuclein were present in GBA1IVS/+ and GBA1IVS/IVS neurons but not in

49

GBA1+/+ neurons, or in GBA1-deficient or GBA1+/+ astrocytes. Transcriptomic analysis of

50

GBA1-deficient neurons and astrocytes revealed cell-type specific profiles. GBA1

51

deficiency in neurons downregulated immune response and upregulated cholesterol

52

synthesis pathways, while GBA1 deficiency in astrocytes downregulated genes

53

associated with translation and upregulated genes involved in extracellular matrix

54

biogenesis. Transcriptomic analysis also suggests that GBA1 deficiency induces

55

neurotoxic reactivity in astrocytes. Together, these findings indicate that GBA1

56

deficiency has cell type-specific effects, with increased neuronal vulnerability to

57

endolysosomal trafficking leading to -synucleinopathy while GBA1 deficiency in

58

astrocytes leads to increased neurotoxic reactivity independent of endolysosomal

59

trafficking and -synucleinopathy.

Introduction
-synucleinopathies, encompassing Parkinson’s disease (PD), dementia with Lewy

63

bodies (DLB), and multiple system atrophy (MSA), are together the second most

64

common group of neurodegenerative disorders following Alzheimer's disease (AD). Like

65

AD and other neurodegenerative disorders, -synucleinopathies are characterized by

66

the neuropathological hallmark of intracellular proteinaceous aggregates, specifically -

67

synuclein oligomers. These aggregates localize intraneuronally in PD and DLB, and are

68

intraglial in MSA. Impairment in multiple cellular processes such as autophagy and

69

mitophagy have been implicated in the process leading to accumulation of aberrant -

70

synuclein1,2. However, the precise mechanisms by which abnormal -synuclein

71

processing contribute to neurodegeneration remain unclear.

72

Heterozygous variants in the glucosylceramidase beta 1 (GBA1) gene, encoding the

73

lysosomal lipid metabolism enzyme glucocerebrosidase, are the strongest genetic risk

74

factor for PD and DLB, with odds ratios between 3 and 203-6. GBA1 variants are not

75

uncommon as 5-15% of PD patients are found to carry a GBA1 variant6-8. GBA1

76

variants are not only associated with increased risk but also accelerated clinical

77

progression of both motor and cognitive symptoms in PD9-11.

78

Our prior work in a Drosophila model of GBA1 deficiency demonstrated altered

79

extracellular vesicle biogenesis resulting in the accelerated spread of protein

80

aggregation, implicating the endolysosomal trafficking system12-14. Endolysosomal

81

trafficking is a complex and highly regulated intracellular vesicular system that

82

encompasses internalization of extracellular cargo targeted to specific organelles,

83

recycling and degradation of intracellular proteins and cargo, a secretion of cargo into

84

the extracellular space, either packaged within membrane bound extracellular vesicles

85

or directly into the extracellular matrix15. Impaired endolysosomal trafficking is implicated

86

in the pathogenesis of neurodegenerative diseases, including AD and PD1,12-14,16,17 and

87

is largely unexplored in how the processing of pathogenic proteins in different cell types

88

influences neurodegeneration. Interestingly, the efficiency of endolysosomal trafficking

89

is tailored to specific cell types, where cells with phagocytic activity such as microglia

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.01.12.697563; this version posted January 13, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. This article is a US Government work. It is not subject to copyright under 17 USC
105 and is also made available for use under a CC0 license.

90

and astrocytes have been found to have more efficient endocytic trafficking of cargo,

91

including -synuclein compared to neurons2,18.

92

Although astrocytes have lower endogenous expression of -synuclein19, -

93

synucleinopathy is found in both neurons and astrocytes in PD and DLB, and

94

predominantly in glia in MSA20. In vitro studies have demonstrated that astrocytes can

95

internalize a-synuclein oligomers more rapidly than neurons2,21,22, and network analysis

96

of proteomic data suggests that astrocytic endolysosomal processing of -synuclein

97

differs from neuronal processing23. These studies suggest that endolysosomal trafficking

98

can differ in capacity in specific cell types, which could have implications on cell type-

99

specific susceptibility to neurodegeneration.

100

To investigate whether GBA1 deficiency has a differential effect on endolysosomal

101

trafficking in neurons and glia, we generated both dopaminergic midbrain neurons and

102

astrocytes from control and GBA1-deficient human iPSCs carrying a null mutation.

103

Interestingly, we found that GBA1 deficiency had a significant impact on early

104

endosome, recycling endosome, and lysosome morphology in neurons but not

105

astrocytes. The protein expression of early endosome antigen 1 (EEA1)- a marker for

106

early endosomes and Rab11- a small GTPase protein involved in vesicular trafficking

107

and recycling endosomes, was also increased in GBA1-deficient neurons but not

108

astrocytes. The expression of lysosome-associated membrane protein 1 (LAMP1)- a

109

protein mainly found on lysosome and late endosomes, was increased in GBA1-

110

deficient neurons and astrocytes with complete knockout of GBA1. Pathologic

111

phosphorylated and oligomeric -synuclein was only detected in GBA1-deficient

112

neurons, not astrocytes. These results indicate that GBA1-deficiency perturbs

113

endolysosomal trafficking and α-synuclein processing in a cell-specific manner.

114

Transcriptomic profiling of GBA1-deficient neurons and astrocytes surprisingly

115

revealed minimal overlap of differentially expressed genes in astrocytes and neurons,

116

indicating more divergent cell-type specific alterations than expected. Expression of

117

genes involved in cholesterol metabolism and immune response were specifically

118

altered in GBA1-deficient neurons, while genes involved in extracellular matrix and

119

translation were specifically dysregulated in astrocytes. Together, our findings indicate

120

that GBA1 deficiency drives specific disease-relevant pathogenic processes in neurons

(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. This article is a US Government work. It is not subject to copyright under 17 USC

121

and astrocytes, linking impaired endolysosomal trafficking to -synucleinopathy in

122

neurons, and promoting reactive transcriptional changes in astrocytes independent of -

123

synucleinopathy in astrocytes. Elucidating how GBA1 deficiency impacts cell-specific

124

functions of neurons and glia is critical for better understanding the complex

125

pathogenesis of -synucleinopathies and developing novel therapeutic cell-type specific

126

strategies to mitigate neurodegeneration.

133

136

141

Discussion

376

The underlying cellular mechanisms responsible for the association between GBA1

377

deficiency and both PD/DLB risk and faster disease progression have been difficult to

378

elucidate. Our study using human iPSC-derived neurons and astrocytes reveals a cell

379

type specific differential effect of GBA1 deficiency, where neurons are more susceptible

380

to perturbation in endolysosomal trafficking and -synucleinopathy compared to

381

astrocytes. We found that early endosomes, lysosomes, and recycling endosomes were

382

all enlarged in cellular compartment size in GBA1-deficient neurons. However, only

383

treatment with CBE of GBA1+/+ astrocytes resulted in enlarged lysosome cellular

384

compartment size, while GBA1-deficient astrocytes did not significantly differ in early or

385

recycling endosome or lysosome compartment size compared to GBA1+/+ astrocytes.

386

These differences correlate with the presence of pathogenic -synuclein aggregation

387

and phosphorylation in neurons but not astrocytes. Transcriptomic profiling of GBA1-

388

deficient neurons and astrocytes confirmed cell-type specific effects, where the majority

389

of DEGs in GBA1-deficient neurons were downregulated, while the majority of DEGs in

390

GBA1-deficient astrocytes were upregulated. Upregulated DEGs in GBA1-deficient

391

neurons included genes in cholesterol synthesis and lipid metabolism pathways, and

392

downregulation of genes involved in inflammatory pathways. In contrast, GBA1-

393

deficient astrocytes had upregulation of genes involved in extracellular matrix

394

biogenesis, neurotoxic astrocyte reactivity, and downregulation of genes involved in

395

translational machinery. These findings suggest that dopaminergic neurons are more

105 and is also made available for use under a CC0 license.

396

susceptible to endolysosomal trafficking defects due to GBA1-deficieny, leading to -

397

synucleinopathy. However, GBA1-deficiency may have a deleterious impact on

398

astrocytes that is independent of -synucleinopathy.

399

Prior work has found enlarged lysosomes and autophagolysosomes in multiple

400

models of GBA1-deficiency33,34 and human postmortem tissue35. However, early

401

endosome defects have not been characterized in GBA1-deficient neurons. Both

402

GBA1IVS/+ and GBA1IVS/IVS neurons were found to have an enlarged early endosome

403

compartment size and increased protein expression of EEA1 by Western blot, even

404

though EEA1 mRNA expression was reduced in GBA1IVS/+ neurons compared to

405

GBA1+/+ neurons. These findings suggest that GBA1 deficiency reduces the fission

406

and/or fusion of early endosomes, resulting in accumulation of enlarged early

407

endosomes and EEA1 protein. Similar alterations in lysosomes and recycling

408

endosomes were found in GBA1IVS/+ and GBA1IVS/IVS neurons. Surprisingly, we did not

409

observe enlarged compartment size of early endosomes, lysosomes or recycling

410

endosomes in GBA1IVS/+, GBA1IVS/IVS or GBA1+/+ astrocytes treated with the GCase

411

inhibitor CBE. Together, our findings indicate that endolysosomal trafficking in neurons

412

is more susceptible to GBA1 deficiency than astrocytes, although when GCase activity

413

is severely reduced or inhibited, endolysosomal trafficking is also impaired in astrocytes.

414

The increased susceptibility of neurons to effects of GBA1 deficiency on

415

endolysosomal trafficking is correlated with the presence of pathogenic -synuclein

416

phosphorylation and aggregation. Human postmortem tissue and in vitro models

417

suggest that phosphorylation of Ser129 may promote pathogenic -synuclein

418

aggregation36,37. Recent work revealed phosphorylation of Ser129 impairs interactions

419

between the ER and mitochondrial membranes, leading to mitochondrial dysfunction

420

and calcium dysregulation38. Interestingly, pathogenic forms of -synuclein were

421

detected in our GBA1-deficient neurons but not astrocytes and transcriptomic analysis

422

of our GBA1-deficient neurons revealed upregulation in genes involved in ER-

423

mitochondrial membrane interactions. Further elucidation of how GBA1 deficiency

424

regulates phosphorylation of Ser129 of -synuclein and perturbs mitochondrial-

425

associated membrane interactions could provide insight into the pathogenesis of -

426

synucleinopathies.

427

Several studies have suggested that astrocytes may have more efficient

428

endolysosomal trafficking compared to neurons, which could be protective due to

429

increased astrocytic endocytosis of extracellular pathologic aggregating proteins that

430

may otherwise seed pathology in neurons21,22,39. Astrocytes differentiated from human

431

iPSCs were found to more rapidly internalize extracellular -synuclein compared to

432

iPSC-dopaminergic neurons. When astrocytes were co-cultured with neurons with

433

biallelic mutations in ATP13A2, which cause a recessive form of Parkinsonism, there

434

was reduced neuronal -synuclein, although phosphorylated and aggregated forms of

435

-synuclein were not examined in this study22. In Drosophila, GBA1 was shown to be

436

necessary for astrocyte-mediated neuronal pruning during sleep40. These differential

437

cell type-specific effects in GBA1 function may have a protective role as well, as

438

expression of wildtype dGBA1b in Drosophila astrocytes was sufficient to prevent

439

protein aggregation in neurons in GBA1-deficient flies40. Our findings further support a

440

cell type-specific role for GBA1, in which neurons have increased susceptibility to

441

endolysosomal trafficking dysfunction and development of -synucleinopathy.

442

While most investigations have used models heterozygous for the more common

443

pathogenic GBA1 variants N370S or L444P, this study examines iPSC-derived neurons

444

and astrocytes carrying the GBA1 IVS2+1 variant as it is considered to be a variant

445

leading to complete absence of GCase protein, rather than a missense variant that may

446

cause additional cellular effects through protein misfolding and ER stress. The effects

447

that we observed in our GBA1IVS/+ neurons trended towards increased severity in

448

homozygous GBA1IVS/IVS neurons and GBA1+/+ neurons treated with CBE, supporting

449

reduced GCase function rather than a gain of function etiology for the observed

450

alterations in endolysosomal trafficking and -synuclein pathology. However, it will be

451

interesting to see if iPSC-derived cells carrying other GBA1 variants such as N370S and

452

L444P recapitulate our cell-type specific findings.

453

RNA-seq analysis of our GBA1-deficient neurons and astrocytes revealed

454

dramatically different gene expression signatures, supporting our findings that GBA1

455

deficiency has cell type-specific effects on divergent cellular pathways. GBA1-deficient

456

neurons were found to have upregulation of genes involved in exocytic, synaptic, and

457

mitochondrial membranes and down-regulation of inflammatory genes. In contrast,

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.01.12.697563; this version posted January 13, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint

458

GBA1-deficient astrocytes resulted in downregulation of genes involved in transcription

459

and upregulation of extracellular matrix genes, suggestive of astrocyte reactivity leading

460

to gliosis. Profiling of GBA1-deficient astrocytes based on markers for astrocyte

461

subpopulations revealed the upregulation of genes consistent with A1 reactive

462

astrocytes32. Neurotoxic A1 astrocytes are implicated in PD pathogenesis through

463

microglial activation, leading to dopaminergic cell death41. GBA1 deficiency has been

464

found to reduce inflammatory response in primary astrocytes from GBA1 D409V knock

465

in mice42, and interestingly, these astrocytes did not have impaired degradation of -

466

synuclein monomers or fibrils. In vitro studies examining the inflammatory response of

467

our GBA1 IVS2+1 mutant astrocytes and co-culturing with wildtype versus GBA1-

468

deficient neurons will elucidate the role of GBA1 deficient astrocytes in PD

469

pathogenesis. However, our transcriptomic analysis suggests that GBA1 deficiency may

470

independently promote the transition of astrocytes into the pathogenic A1 state,

471

contributing to pathogenesis independently of endolysosomal trafficking defects and -

472

synuclein aggregation.

473

Glucocerebrosidase is an important enzyme for ceramide and glucosphingosine

474

metabolism, and several studies have underscored the influence of lipid metabolism on

475

neurodegeneration. Ceramidase inhibition with carmofur, resulting in increased

476

ceramide level, was found to reverse -synuclein aggregation in GBA1-deficient

477

HEK293T cells43. Given that astrocytes are the main cell type in the central nervous

478

system responsible for lipid metabolism and efflux of lipids to neurons 18, we anticipated

479

that GBA1 deficiency would have a significant impact on astrocyte lipid metabolism.

480

Interestingly, there was significant upregulation of genes involved in lipid metabolism

481

pathways, including cholesterol efflux, fatty acid metabolism, inflammatory lipid

482

signaling, and membrane remodeling (Fig 6,8). Alterations in lipid metabolism in both

483

neurons and glia have been implicated in PD, neurodegeneration, and aging44-46. Our

484

findings support differential lipid metabolism alterations due to GBA1 deficiency in

485

neurons and astrocytes, and further characterization of lipid profiles will elucidate how

486

alterations in each cell type influences neurodegeneration.

487

Our model of PD-GBA1 deficiency using iPSC differentiated neurons and astrocytes

488

reveals the cell-specific effects and potential neuroprotective roles for astrocyte specific

489

GBA1. Importantly, this study underscores the role that glial cells have in PD and how

490

known risk factors like GBA1 may impair glial function independently of -synuclein

491

aggregation, greatly contributing to the progression of neurodegeneration. Although

492

GBA1 deficiency in astrocytes did not result in endolysosomal trafficking defects and -

493

synucleinopathy as in GBA1-deficient neurons, transcriptomic profiling suggests GBA1-

494

deficiency causes pathogenic reactive astrocyte changes and extracellular matrix

495

changes. These extracellular matrix changes may represent gliosis28. Our findings lay a

496

strong foundation to investigate further mechanisms that underlie GBA1-deficient

497

pathology and could reveal novel therapeutic targets for slowing progression of

498

neurodegeneration in a broader population of -synucleinopathy patients.

500

Conclusion

501

In conclusion, our study examined endolysosomal trafficking in human iPSCs

502

carrying the GBA1 IVS2+1 null variant differentiated into dopaminergic neurons and

503

astrocytes. In this model, we demonstrated cell-type specific enlargement of the early

504

endosome, lysosome, and recycling endosome compartment in GBA1-deficient neurons

505

but not astrocytes. GBA1-deficient neurons but not astrocytes were found to have -

506

synucleinopathy. RNA sequencing reveals cell type-specific effects of GBA1 deficiency,

507

with significantly different cellular pathways affected in GBA1-deficient neurons and

508

GBA1-deficient astrocytes. Our model provides insight into the cell-type specific role of

509

GBA1-deficiency and will be valuable for further defining the individual contributions of

510

cell types to -synucleinopathy and neurodegeneration.
